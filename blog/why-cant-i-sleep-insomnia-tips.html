Why You Can't Sleep Even When Exhausted — And How to Fix It

By University of Today  ·  5 min read  ·  Sleep Health

You are exhausted all day — but the moment your head hits the pillow, your brain switches on. You lie there, watching the minutes tick past, dreading the alarm that is coming too soon. If this sounds familiar, you are not broken. But you do need to understand what is actually happening.

The Real Reason You Cannot Sleep Even When You Are Exhausted

The most common misconception about insomnia is that it is a "sleep problem." It is not. It is a nervous system problem.

Your body has two operating modes: sympathetic (fight or flight) and parasympathetic (rest and digest). Sleep can only happen in parasympathetic mode. But most people spend the entire day — and evening — in sympathetic mode, driven by work pressure, screen stimulation, caffeine, and unresolved anxiety.

By the time they get into bed, their cortisol is still elevated, their heart rate is above its resting level, and their brain is in problem-solving mode. The body is physically incapable of sleeping in this state — no matter how tired it is.

The 7 Most Common Reasons You Cannot Sleep

1. High Cortisol at Bedtime

Cortisol is meant to be high in the morning and low at night. Modern lifestyles — late work, evening emails, stressful news — keep cortisol elevated well past sunset. Solution: begin an active wind-down routine 45–60 minutes before bed that specifically lowers cortisol (breathwork is the fastest tool for this).

2. Screen Use Before Bed

Blue light from phones and laptops suppresses melatonin — your sleep hormone — by up to 50%. But the bigger problem is stimulation. Social media, news, and video content keep your brain in alert mode. Stop screens 60 minutes before bed. If you must use your phone, switch to night mode and use it for audio content only.

3. Sleeping at the Wrong Time

Your body has a natural "sleep window" driven by your circadian rhythm — typically between 10pm and midnight for most people. If you try to sleep significantly outside this window, melatonin is not properly released, and sleep onset is much harder. Aligning your bedtime with your natural circadian window dramatically reduces how long it takes to fall asleep.

4. Unresolved Mental Load

The "to-do list brain" at bedtime is one of the most common sleep complaints. Your brain has been processing information all day and uses the quiet of bedtime to surface unfinished business. Solution: do a "brain dump" — write down everything on your mind before getting into bed. This externalises the mental load and reduces pre-sleep rumination significantly.

5. Caffeine Timing

Caffeine has a 5–7 hour half-life. If you drink coffee at 3pm, 50% of it is still active at 9pm. Many people who believe they are "immune to caffeine" are actually still being sleep-disrupted by it — just not noticeably so. Try cutting off caffeine by noon for two weeks and observe the difference.

Adenosine is the chemical that builds up in your brain during the day and creates "sleep pressure." Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors — so the pressure builds behind a dam. When caffeine wears off, it floods in all at once, causing the afternoon crash. The side effect is disrupted sleep architecture at night.

6. Temperature

Your body needs to drop its core temperature by 1–2°F to initiate sleep. Many bedrooms are too warm for optimal sleep. The ideal sleeping temperature is between 65–68°F (18–20°C). A warm bath 60–90 minutes before bed paradoxically helps — as your body cools down afterwards, it mimics the natural temperature drop of sleep onset.

7. Anxiety About Not Sleeping (Sleep Anxiety)

One of the cruelest aspects of insomnia: the more you worry about not sleeping, the harder it is to sleep. This creates a feedback loop where the bed becomes associated with wakefulness and anxiety rather than rest. Cognitive techniques and guided meditation can break this cycle by changing your relationship with sleep from anxious effort to effortless release.

What Actually Works: The Evidence-Based Approach

The most effective non-pharmaceutical treatments for insomnia — all supported by clinical research — are:

  • Controlled breathwork — directly lowers cortisol and activates the parasympathetic nervous system
  • Yoga and gentle movement — releases physical tension and drops the body's arousal level
  • Guided meditation — breaks the cycle of sleep anxiety and trains the mind to let go
  • Sleep timing alignment — syncing bedtime with your circadian window
  • Consistent sleep schedule — the anchor that holds all other improvements in place

You do not need medication. You do not need a fancy mattress. You need to understand your nervous system — and learn how to work with it rather than against it.
